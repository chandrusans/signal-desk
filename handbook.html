Chapter 01What this is

Signal Desk is a research tool for a self-directed retail investor doing swing trading in liquid US equities. It watches a small pinned list of tickers, pulls their live prices from Yahoo Finance every 30 seconds, computes a set of real technical indicators, weighs the news flow around each name, and gives every ticker a composite score from 0 to 100.

It also proposes a trade plan — where to enter, where to place your stop, where to take profit — computed from live volatility, not from a hardcoded number. And it tells you when to not trade, because most of the time no clean setup exists.

The single most important idea

The desk is not telling you what to buy. It's screening the tape and producing an opinion. You keep the decision. The tool's job is to make the reasoning transparent so you can disagree with it intelligently.

Chapter 02Dashboard tour

Top to bottom on the main page:

  1. Masthead + pulse strip — page-wide status. Feed health (live / stale / error), last update time, S&P/Nasdaq/DXY/rates, and — most importantly — the regime cell showing the current VIX level and what it allows.
  2. Top candidates — up to 5 pinned tickers, reordered every poll so the highest-scoring pick sits at rank 01. Each card shows current price, intraday change, computed setup state, the composite score, and a 14-day sparkline.
  3. Detail panel — click any card to expand. Shows the setup and regime callout, transparent score breakdown, live indicators (SMAs, RSI, ATR, returns), an ATR-derived trade plan, and a full exit checklist.
  4. Strategy rulebook — the six mechanical rules the framework operates by (universe, entry, stop, target, sizing, regime).
  5. Political calendar — curated market-moving events (FOMC, elections, tariff reviews) with days-until countdown.
  6. Market intelligence — live news feed pulled from Yahoo Finance, with sentiment scores per headline and a POL badge on political ones.
  7. Backtest harness — pick a strategy variant + universe + window; see equity curve and stats.
  8. Screener — filter a 20-ticker universe by score, market cap, P/E, momentum, sentiment, and setup state.
  9. Data source architecture — what feeds are live vs still mock.
  10. Disclosure — the not-investment-advice paragraph. Read it.

Chapter 03The composite score

The composite score is a number from 0 to 100 that summarizes everything the desk knows about a stock. Higher is better. It's computed from real market data on each poll, so it changes as the tape moves.

The score is the sum of five base components plus two overlay adjustments:

ComponentWhat it measuresMax
Trend Alignment of price vs moving averages (MA20, MA50, MA200) and the direction of the 20-day MA. Full alignment (price > MA20 > MA50 > MA200) plus upward MA slope = maximum. 30 pts
Momentum 20-day price return, adjusted for RSI. Strong 20d gains score high; overbought RSI (>75) or oversold (<30) applies a penalty. 25 pts
Location How close price is to the 52-week high. At/near the high = full points; more than 20% below = zero. 20 pts
Volume Ratio of 5-day average volume to 20-day average volume. Expanding volume (>1.3×) = accumulation, scores high. 15 pts
Volatility 14-day ATR as a % of price. The tradable sweet spot is 1.5–4%; too quiet or too wild scores less. 10 pts
News catalyst Overlay. Live headline sentiment (keyword-scored) from the last 24 hours. More headlines with stronger sentiment moves this more. ±10 pts
Political overlay Overlay. Political headlines are scored using a sector-aware lexicon — "chips act" is bullish for semis but neutral for healthcare, "antitrust" is bearish for big tech, etc. ±8 pts

The base components (trend + momentum + location + volume + volatility) sum to a maximum of 100. The overlays can push the score up or down by an additional 18 combined. The final score is clamped to the 0–100 range.

How to read the score

  • 75+ — every dimension is firing. This is what an A+ setup looks like. Rare.
  • 55–74 — strong trend + momentum, worth watching. May trade if the setup state is BREAKOUT or PULLBACK.
  • 35–54 — mixed. Something is missing (probably trend alignment or volume). Not a trade — a watchlist item.
  • Below 35 — broken or basing. Skip.
Score is not permission to trade

A high score means the tape looks good. It doesn't mean the current bar is a valid entry. Always check the setup state below the score — it's what tells you whether an entry actually exists.

Chapter 04Setup states

Each ticker is classified into one of six mechanical setup states. This tells you what action, if any, the framework thinks is appropriate right now.

BREAKOUT Price is at a 20-day high on expanding volume (5d/20d ratio ≥ 1.2×), RSI is between 55 and 72, and the trend is aligned. This is the cleanest momentum entry — enter at market. Full sizing.

PULLBACK Trend is intact, price has pulled back to within 2% of the 10-day moving average, RSI has cooled to 40–58, and 20-day return is still positive. This is buying the dip inside an uptrend. Enter on close within the pullback zone.

EARLY Uptrend is forming (price above MA20 and MA50) but no confirmed breakout or pullback entry yet. Watchlist only. Don't enter blindly — wait for the setup to graduate to BREAKOUT or PULLBACK.

EXTENDED Price is more than 6% above the MA20 or RSI is above 78. Chasing here has poor risk/reward. Wait for a pullback to MA10/20 before considering entry.

BROKEN Price is below MA20 or MA50. Trend is broken. Skip until the stock builds a new base and reclaims the moving averages on volume.

AVOID Nothing clean. No entry criteria met. Do nothing.

Most days, the majority of tickers show EARLY, BROKEN, or AVOID. That's normal — clean setups are rare. The system rejecting a trade is doing its job.

Chapter 05Reading a trade plan

When you open a pick's detail panel, the trade plan block shows four cells:

  • Entry band — the price range to enter within. For BREAKOUT setups it's tight around current price; for PULLBACK it's a zone around the MA10.
  • Stop — where you cut the loss, no exceptions. Calculated as max(1.5 × ATR₁₄, 5%) below entry, capped at 10%.
  • Target — where you take profit. Set at +2.5R above entry, meaning the reward is 2.5× the risk you took.
  • Time stop — 15 trading days. If the trade is flat after three weeks, close it — opportunity cost.

The R-multiple system

All targets and stops are measured in "R" — the initial risk you took. If your entry was $100 and your stop was $95, then R = $5. A +2R gain means the trade is up $10. A +2.5R target means you're aiming for $12.50.

This is the professional way to size trades — instead of fixed dollar profits, everything is expressed relative to your original risk. A 60% win rate at 2.5R average wins is highly profitable even after losses.

The exit checklist

Every detail panel includes a mechanical exit checklist:

  1. Hard stop hit → exit full position, no averaging down.
  2. Price hits +1R → trail stop to breakeven.
  3. Price hits +2R → trail stop to +1R.
  4. Target hit → scale half, trail remainder under MA20.
  5. Flat after 15 trading days → close (opportunity cost).
  6. SPY closes below its 50-day MA → exit entire momentum book.
Position sizing

The framework uses risk-parity: risk 0.75% of capital per trade. Shares = (capital × 0.0075) / stop distance. Wider ATR → smaller position. Every trade risks the same dollar amount regardless of how volatile the stock is.

Chapter 06Regime filter · VIX

The VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) measures expected market volatility. The desk pulls it live and uses it to gate what setups it will trade.

  • VIX < 15 · Calm. Full sizing. All setup types allowed.
  • VIX 15–20 · Normal. Full sizing but only A+ setups (BREAKOUT and PULLBACK).
  • VIX 20–25 · Elevated. Half sizing. PULLBACK entries only — no chasing breakouts into vol.
  • VIX 25–35 · Stress. Quarter sizing at most. No new longs. Manage existing risk.
  • VIX > 35 · Crisis. No new positions. Exit weakest holdings. Hedge the book.

The regime cell in the pulse strip shows the current VIX and what the framework will let you do. The detail panel also shows an explicit "Regime blocks new entry on this setup" line when a candidate's setup isn't allowed under the current regime.

Chapter 07Political overlay

Political events move stocks materially. Tariffs, sanctions, drug-pricing bills, antitrust rulings, FOMC decisions — these can matter more than any technical setup for weeks at a time.

Every headline in the news feed is scanned for political trigger words (tariff, antitrust, fomc, sanctions, chips act, congress, etc.). If any match, the headline gets a POL badge in the intel feed.

Sector-aware sentiment

Political sentiment is not a global bull/bear score — the same word can mean opposite things depending on the sector. Each pinned ticker is tagged with a political sector:

  • semis — NVDA, AMD, ASML, MRVL. "Chips act" bullish, "export controls" bearish.
  • defense — PLTR, CAT. "Budget increase" and "conflict" bullish, "peace talks" bearish.
  • energy — XOM. "Drilling permit" bullish, "climate rules" bearish.
  • healthcare — LLY, NVAX. "FDA approval" bullish, "drug pricing" bearish.
  • tech-mega — META, MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN, CRWD. "Antitrust" bearish, "settled" bullish.
  • crypto — HOOD, COIN. "Spot ETF approved" bullish, "SEC crackdown" bearish.
  • ev — TSLA, F. "Tax credit" bullish, "credit removed" bearish.

The political overlay adjusts the composite score by up to ±8 points based on the volume and average sentiment of political headlines in the last 24 hours, weighted through the ticker's sector lexicon.

Chapter 08The screener

Below the fold, the screener runs the same composite scoring against a 20-ticker universe (mix of megacaps, momentum names, sectors). Use it when the pinned picks aren't showing setups you like — sort by score descending and see what's actually setting up in the wider market.

Filter controls:

  • Sector — GICS-style sector filter (Semiconductors, Software, Cybersecurity, etc.)
  • Min composite score — drop everything below your threshold
  • Min market cap — currently static; live cap requires a fundamentals API (see data sources)
  • Max P/E — currently static
  • Min momentum (20d) — live, computed from real bars
  • Min sentiment — currently static

Every column header is clickable to sort. Setup states are shown as chips in the Setup column.

Chapter 09Political calendar

A curated list of upcoming events that historically move markets. Auto-hides past events and sorts by nearest first. Each event shows:

  • Days until — countdown in the leftmost slot; TODAY when it hits.
  • Tag — FOMC / ELECTION / CHINA / FISCAL / GOVSHUT / MACRO.
  • Note — which sectors typically react and why.

The impact colour on the countdown (red = high impact, amber = medium) tells you how much attention to pay. A high-impact event within 5 days is a good reason to reduce sizing on affected sectors regardless of what the technicals say.

Chapter 11Glossary

  • ATR — Average True Range. A measure of a stock's typical daily price movement in dollars. Used to size stops so they respect the stock's natural volatility.
  • RSI — Relative Strength Index. Momentum oscillator from 0 to 100. Above 70 = overbought, below 30 = oversold. Between 40 and 70 is where healthy trends live.
  • SMA — Simple Moving Average. Average closing price over N days. Trend-following indicator.
  • MA alignment — When shorter MAs sit above longer ones (price > MA20 > MA50 > MA200). Confirms an uptrend across timeframes.
  • R-multiple — Trade result expressed as a multiple of initial risk. A +2R winner made twice what you were willing to lose.
  • R:R ratio — Reward-to-risk ratio. A 3:1 setup risks $1 to make $3.
  • Regime — The current volatility environment, quantified by VIX. Dictates position sizing and which setups are eligible.
  • Composite score — The 0–100 rank Signal Desk assigns each ticker, summing across trend / momentum / location / volume / volatility / catalysts / political.
  • Setup state — A mechanical classification of what action, if any, is appropriate: BREAKOUT, PULLBACK, EARLY, EXTENDED, BROKEN, AVOID.
  • FOMC — Federal Open Market Committee. The Fed body that sets interest rates. Meetings are the biggest scheduled market-moving events.
  • Section 301 — US trade-law tool for imposing tariffs. Reviews typically move semis, EV, and solar stocks.

Chapter 12Dashboard · your positions

The Dashboard is your personal portfolio view. Anything you actually own goes here; the desk then tracks it live and — most importantly — tells you when a seasoned PM would sell it. This isn't a P&L calculator. It's a decision-support layer for every open position.

Adding a position

Fill the Add form with ticker, share count, buy price, buy date, and optionally a manual stop. Everything is stored locally in your browser (no server, no account). Use Export JSON regularly if you care about the data — clear browser data and it's gone.

If you don't set a stop, the desk defaults to 5% below entry — a reasonable equity swing default. Override it any time via the Edit button.

The verdict system

Every position gets one of four verdicts, refreshed on each poll (15s). Verdicts are colour-coded on the card's left rail and sorted so the ones needing attention float to the top:

  • SELL Exit the whole position. Reasons that trigger it: hard stop hit, setup broken with a loss, regime turned stress/crisis, time stop with no follow-through after 15 days.
  • TRIM Take partial profit. Reasons: RSI overbought (>78) on a >2R winner, setup went EXTENDED on a winner, MA20 slope negative on a winner.
  • HOLD Do nothing yet. Fresh positions, or profitable positions with trend intact. The reason line will tell you where to trail your stop.
  • WATCH Decision imminent. Price is within a fraction of R of your stop or target — be at your screen.

How the sell logic thinks

The verdict function checks rules in priority order and returns the first match:

  1. Hard stop hit — no exceptions, no averaging down.
  2. Regime turned (VIX > 25) — protect the whole book.
  3. Setup broken with loss — trend dead, cut before it compounds.
  4. Time stop — 15 trading days with less than 1R gain means the thesis didn't materialize. Free the capital for a better setup.
  5. Overextended winner — RSI > 78 with a >2R gain, or setup gone EXTENDED. Scale half, move stop up.
  6. Trend deteriorating on a winner — MA20 slope negative with >1R gain. Protect gains before they roundtrip.
  7. Fresh position — first 3 days, always HOLD to give the setup room.
  8. Winner with intact trend — HOLD, trail stop per R-multiple gained.
  9. Near stop or target — WATCH.
Personalization

You can override any position's stop via the Edit button. The verdict engine will re-evaluate against your custom stop on the next poll. Notes fields (planned) let you tag entries with your thesis so future-you knows why past-you bought it.

Portfolio summary strip

Above the position list: portfolio value, unrealized P&L in $ and %, position count, count of action alerts (sell + trim), and current regime. When SELL or TRIM alerts > 0, the alert cell turns red — a nudge to open the dashboard.

Import / Export / Clear

Export JSON downloads your full portfolio as a plain JSON file — commit it to a private repo if you want history. Import reads a previously-exported file and replaces the current portfolio (with confirmation). Clear wipes everything after confirmation. These give you data portability without any server dependency.

Data lives only in your browser

Positions are stored in localStorage under key sd-portfolio-v1. Clearing site data, switching browsers, or using incognito will make them disappear. Export before doing any of that.

Chapter 13Options · Robinhood & Schwab

The Options tab surfaces up to 10 single-leg call or put contracts ranked for the two brokers you actually use: Robinhood and Charles Schwab. Contract details, entry, exits, stop, breakeven, and max loss are all pre-computed. The scored contracts are the ones a discretionary trader would spend time inspecting.

Data source

NASDAQ's public options-chain JSON endpoint (no API key). Refreshed on button click. For each ticker, ~2000 raw contracts get scored, filtered, and the single best short-term and long-term contract per direction is returned.

Scoring model

Each contract is scored from 0–100 across five factors. Contracts that fail any hard filter (e.g. under 50 open interest, spread wider than 20%) are excluded entirely — not shown at any score:

  • DTE fit (30 pts) — short-term sweet spot is 21–35 days to expiration; long-term is 120–270. Farther from the sweet spot loses points.
  • Moneyness (25 pts) — slightly OTM (strike/spot 1.00–1.10 for calls, inverse for puts) is the sweet spot for R-multiple efficiency.
  • Open Interest (20 pts) — ≥1000 is a clean market, ≥200 is fine, under 50 is filtered out.
  • Spread (15 pts) — bid-ask under 5% is tight; over 20% is filtered out (you'll get sliced on the fill).
  • Volume today (10 pts) — trailing activity confirms real liquidity right now.

What each card shows

  • Direction chip — CALL (green) or PUT (red) with a matching left rail.
  • Contract line — strike, expiry date, DTE, short-term vs long-term tag.
  • Bid / ask / mid — the mid is your limit-order price. Do not use market orders on options.
  • Per-contract premium — mid × 100. This is what you pay for one contract.
  • Estimated delta — from moneyness (not Black-Scholes). Ballpark for choosing between contracts.
  • Liquidity rating — good / okay / thin. Prefer good.
  • Copy OCC symbol — one click copies e.g. NVDA260814C00215000. Paste that into Robinhood's or Schwab's search bar and the contract opens directly, no clicking through the chain.
  • + Track — writes the contract to the Dashboard as an option position with strike / expiry / direction / multiplier=100 so it shows up next to your stock positions with P&L on premium.

The analytics strip · six cells you actually trade off

  • Entry (limit) — the mid price. Submit as a limit order.
  • Stop (−50%) — exit if premium halves. This is the mechanical stop for long options; most books use −50% because premium can decay fast.
  • Target +50% — premium goes to 1.5× entry. Underlying price required is estimated using delta.
  • Target +100% — premium doubles. Same estimation caveat.
  • Breakeven at expiry — the underlying price where you break even if held to expiration. For calls: strike + premium. For puts: strike − premium.
  • Max loss — premium per contract + $0.65 regulatory fee (both Robinhood and Schwab). This is the whole ballgame if you're wrong.

Broker realities

  • Both apps charge $0.65/contract as a regulatory pass-through. Commission itself is $0 on Robinhood and $0 base on Schwab.
  • Both accept the OCC symbol format used on every card.
  • Both are American-style — assignment risk if in-the-money at expiry. This framework's positions are long-only single-leg, so assignment isn't a concern unless you're the seller (not our recommendation).
Long options can lose 100%

Unlike stocks, a long option can decay to zero even if you're eventually right — theta and time-to-expiry are unforgiving. Never size a position where the max loss is more than you're prepared to lose. Delta on these cards is an estimate; verify greeks in your broker before entering.

Chapter 14Metals page · XAU/USD & XAG/USD

The metals page is a dedicated view for gold and silver. Metals trade on different drivers than equities — Fed rates, dollar strength, real yields, geopolitical risk — so they get their own dashboard rather than sitting inside the equity screener.

Live pricing source

Yahoo Finance does not serve XAUUSD=X or XAGUSD=X directly. The page uses GC=F (COMEX Gold continuous futures) and SI=F (Silver futures) as the live proxy — basis vs spot is under 0.5%, so the projected ranges hold at spot too. Both symbols are displayed to you as XAU/USD and XAG/USD per market convention.

Range projection · the math

For each metal, two ranges are projected off the current price and 14-day ATR:

  • Day range = price ± 0.5 × ATR₁₄. This is a ~1σ band for a normal trading day — about 68% of days close inside it.
  • Week range = price ± ATR₁₄ × √5. Variance scales with the square root of time; a week is roughly 2.24× the daily band, not 5×. A common beginner error is to multiply by 5.

A Fed decision, CPI print, or geopolitical shock can and does widen the actual weekly move well beyond this projection. The band is a base case, not a promise.

Directional bias

Each panel labels the tape as one of four states:

  • BULL BIAS — full MA alignment + MA20 sloping up. Buy pullbacks to MA10/20.
  • CONSTRUCTIVE — above MA20 and MA50 but 200d not confirmed. With-trend only.
  • BEAR BIAS — below MA20 with negative slope. Fade rallies; longs need MA20 reclaim.
  • RANGE — no clear trend. Sell resistance, buy support.

Drivers strip

The top strip shows the four things you need in your peripheral vision when trading metals:

  • DXY — US dollar index. Dollar down = metals up. Inversely coloured (a DXY drop is green because it's a metals tailwind).
  • US 10Y — Treasury yield. Rising yields = headwind for gold (opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding asset). Inversely coloured.
  • VIX — risk regime. High VIX often supports gold's safe-haven bid. Silver is more industrial and can suffer with growth fears.
  • Gold-Silver ratio — XAU / XAG. Above 85 historically means silver is cheap vs gold. Below 60 means silver rich. Traders use extremes for pair trades.

Metals calendar

The events that move metals most: FOMC, CPI, NFP, Jackson Hole, US elections. Countdown shows days-until; impact colour flags priority. High-impact events within 5 days = reduce size regardless of technicals.

Trade plan

Same framework as equities but tuned for metals volatility: stop = max(1.5 × ATR, 2%), target = +2.5R, time stop = 15 days. Metals have lower percentage volatility than stocks so the 2% floor is tighter than the equity 5%.

Not all metals move together

Gold trades on rates + geopolitics. Silver has ~50% industrial demand, so it also cares about global manufacturing PMIs and copper's direction. When gold rips on a Fed cut and silver lags, that's typically a signal to fade silver's underperformance via the gold-silver ratio.
